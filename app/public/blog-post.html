Understanding Different Types of Green Energies

  • Reza Jafery
  • 20 October 2018
  • 3,807
Share Tweet Pin Email

People around the world are beginning to consider renewable energy sources such as solar and wind power as viable alternatives to relying on a power grid. We get more and more questions every week about cost effective ways to set up solar panels, and green energy adoption seems to be on a steady rise.

Renewable Energy, Self Sufficiency, and Living Off the Grid

Maybe you want to get off the grid, become completely self-sufficient, live independent from government funded power sources, or turn your home into a source of income through generating excess energy— regardless of whatever sparked your curiosity, you’re going to need to understand the basics of renewable energy to get started.

Renewable Energy Sources

Most people are pretty familiar with solar energy, but there are 6 other sources of power that don’t get as much attention from mainstream media. Let’s dive into the different sectors that are beginning to take off so we can get a better understanding of what are options are in the present, and the future.

Solar Power

Solar energy comes from the most reliable entity in the galaxy, the sun! The sun never misses a day of work, it always wakes up at the same time every day, and is the most reliable source of power for anyone living in an area with mostly sunny climates. Solar power is gathered and stored in several different ways- essentially, we collect solar energy and convert its heat to electricity. The most common applications of solar energy are water heating, space heating/cooling, and solar photovoltaics which convert solar heat to electricity.

While initially popularized for the benefits solar power can provide to the environment— recent developments in technology have lowered the cost of implementing solar power so much that it’s now relatively cost effective to convert your home to solar energy, and begin actually earning money every month through generating excess energy. As opposed to paying the government for access to a local power grid.

Wind Power

Another common source of renewable energy is wind power. Have you ever been driving down the highway and seen gigantic towers with propellers on top of them? Those are wind turbines, these are the most common way of collecting wind power.

Wind power isn’t as cost effective as solar power, and typically is used by businesses who can afford to set up large scale wind power facilities. They currently provide around 6% of the energy in the U.S.A.

Hydro Electric Energy

A lot of folks are familiar with Hydro Electric Energy, they just don’t know it! The Hoover Dam and Niagara falls are two of the more widely known Hydro Electric Energy sources, generating around 7.5% of the energy consumed in the United States. Hydro Electric essentially means the power we generate through moving water. The power is created when water moves through a large turbine, creating perpetual motion that lasts, well, as long as the water keeps flowing!

Biomass

Biomass energy is any form of energy that is generated from living organic matter such as animals and plants. It’s considered renewable because, well, plants grow all over the damn place— and I’m not talking Hoover this time! Biomass is interesting because it’s powered by another form of renewable energy- solar power. The sun allows plants to grow, which feeds animals, and both of those can be used to generate biomass energy.

Geothermal Power

Geothermal power is power generated from the heat trapped within the earths crust. It’s only a tiny percentage of the renewable energy currently being used globally, but some countries like Iceland rely heavily on it to heat their homes. Geothermal energy is collected by pumping water underground to hot areas, the water then becomes steam and rises. As it rises it enters a wind turbine and spins it- which generates electricity. Geothermal power is only a feasible energy source in certain parts of the world, and currently not heavily relied on in the U.S.
