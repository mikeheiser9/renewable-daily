On the Grid vs. Off the Grid Renewable Energy Systems

  • Reza Jafery
  • 18 October 2018
  • 3
  • 1001
Share Tweet Pin Email

There are tons of different types of renewable energy systems to choose from when deciding on one for your home. When it comes to green energy options that are feasible to install in a typical home, there are solar electric systems, wind electric systems, micro hydropower systems, and hybrid solar/wind electric systems. All of these can be categorized into one of two main categories, these categories represent the key differences in home systems used across the world today.

Before you decide on what source of energy you’re going to use, you need to decide if your system will be On the Grid or Off the Grid.

Although becoming completely self sufficient is appealing to many who look to renewable energy, there are some serious benefits that having an On the Grid solution provides. When we say, “On the grid”, it means that the renewable energy system is connected to the local power and energy provider. The “grid” is just what people in the energy sector call a local power grid— the magical thing that makes our lights turn on and keeps our milk from going bad.

Let’s take a look at both options and the advantages/disadvantages they provide.

On the Grid or Grid-Connected Renewable Energy Systems

Not everyone has the weather necessary to provide solar or wind power year round. Unless you’re in a city that stays sunny 364 days a year, solar power might not be reliable 100% of the time (unless you have some serious batteries to store your power!). Connecting your system to the grid means that whenever you’re not generating enough power to sustain your electrical load, you can still fall back on the grid to supply additional electricity.

Another benefit of being on the grid is being able to take part in net-metering with your local power company. The majority of states have net metering programs, which allow individuals who install renewable energy setups to receive a little incentive for their hard work.

If you’re on the grid, and applicable for net metering, your power company will pay you for excess power that you generate and feed into the grid. If you’re generating more power than you’re consuming, you can feed that energy back into the grid and earn cash. If you have a small setup this might just subsidize your bill, however with a serious enough renewable energy system you can actually earn money every month instead of spending it on electricity bills.

Off the Grid Renewable Energy Systems

Off the grid renewable energy systems, or “stand-alone systems” have their own unique set of advantages and disadvantages. If you’re one of the people whose drawn into renewable energy by the idea of becoming completely independent and self sufficient, this is your only option.

Off-grid power systems are also advantageous if you live in a remote area without power lines. In these cases you either have to pay a utility company to extend a power line to meet your needs, or set up your own stand-alone renewable energy system. One of the obvious disadvantages to being on-grid is that you won’t receive money from your local power company for generating excess energy. Another disadvantage is that these systems typically require more time and effort in actually building/setting up the system. You need to buy batteries, a charge controller, safety equipment, meters, and power conditioning equipment. Building this all yourself also means that you’re going to be the only one to blame if the power goes out— so make sure to study up, and if you’re not confident— don’t be ashamed to call a professional in to help.
